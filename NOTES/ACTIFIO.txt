Opus 4.7 thinking high knows quite a bit about Actifio (both 2009-2012 era and in general)

Rough sketch of the surface area at a company like 2009-2012 Actifio:
•	Data-path / storage core — IO path, on-disk format, dedup, GC, replication. The IP.
•	Application integration — VMware, Oracle, SQL Server, Exchange, SAP capture/mount agents. Big team at Actifio specifically; SLA-driven app capture was their headline marketed feature.
•	Management / orchestration / SLA engine — policies, schedules, the workflow that drove copies and mounts. Substantial.
•	UI, REST API, CLI — its own team.
•	Platform / appliance / installer / upgrade / serviceability — non-trivial for shipping a physical box.
•	Networking targets — FC and iSCSI target stacks, NFS/SMB mount-back paths.
•	QA / test infra / hardware lab — at storage companies this is a real slice of headcount.
•	Sky / cloud variants — emerging in this period.
Realistic split: data-path is maybe 20-30% of engineering at a company like that.


Papers to read:
Ohad Rodeh, "B-trees, Shadowing, and Clones," published in ACM Transactions on Storage (TOS), vol. 3, no. 4, Feb. 2008 (an earlier workshop version appeared at LinuxCon / the 2007 Linux Storage & Filesystem Workshop). It's the foundational paper on copy‑on‑write (a.k.a. "shadowed") B-trees for storage systems, and it's the design basis for btrfs's metadata layer.


Zhu et al. "Avoiding the Disk Bottleneck in the Data Domain Deduplication File System (FAST '08)"
It's important because it's the paper that turned inline deduplication at scale from "theoretically appealing but I/O‑bound" into a shipping product category. Almost every dedup storage system built in the next 10–15 years (including Actifio's) traces its index design back to it.

If you imagine a small bookshelf for "how modern storage systems are built"

Rosenblum & Ousterhout, "The Design and Implementation of a Log‑Structured File System" (SOSP '91) — the LFS paper, which is why segment‑cleaning GC exists.
Quinlan & Dorward, "Venti: a new approach to archival storage" (FAST '02) — the first major content‑addressable archival store.
Muthitacharoen et al., "A Low‑Bandwidth Network File System" (LBFS) (SOSP '01) — variable‑sized chunking via Rabin fingerprints.
Rodeh, "B‑trees, Shadowing, and Clones" (TOS '08) — the CoW B‑tree paper that underpins btrfs and a lot of modern snapshot metadata.
Zhu, Li, Patterson, FAST '08 — the paper that made all of the above usable for production‑scale inline dedup.
